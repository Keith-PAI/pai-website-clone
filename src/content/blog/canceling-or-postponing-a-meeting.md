It takes courage to cancel a meeting. But sometimes it's the most productive thing you can do.

## When to Consider Canceling

- **The agenda is empty** — If you can't fill the agenda, the meeting isn't needed
- **Key decision-makers can't attend** — Holding the meeting without them often means having it again later
- **The issue resolved itself** — Sometimes email or a quick conversation handles it
- **People are overwhelmed** — During crunch times, giving time back is a gift

## When to Postpone Instead

- **Critical information isn't ready** — Better to wait than waste time speculating
- **The right people need more prep time** — A well-prepared meeting beats an improvised one
- **External factors** — weather, travel disruptions, or competing urgent priorities

## How to Cancel Well

- **Communicate early** — As soon as you know, let people know
- **Explain briefly** — A one-sentence reason shows respect
- **Provide alternatives** — "I'll send an email summary instead" or "Let's reconvene next week"
- **Don't apologize excessively** — Protecting people's time is a good thing

## The Culture Shift

Organizations that normalize thoughtful cancellation tend to have better meetings overall. When people know that meetings only happen when they're truly needed, they pay more attention when they do.
