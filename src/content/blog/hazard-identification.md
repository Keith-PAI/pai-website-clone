Part 2 of the Safety Risk Management (SRM) Series Under 14 CFR Part 5. Hazard identification represents the critical juncture where comprehension of system function and operational context transforms into a systematic approach for recognizing conditions that diminish safety margins.

## Defining Hazards

**FAA Definition (14 CFR 5.3):** "A condition or object that could foreseeably cause or contribute to an incident or aircraft accident."

**ICAO Definition (Doc 9859, 4th Edition):** "A condition or an object with the potential to cause or contribute to an aircraft accident or incident."

A hazard is distinct from the negative event itself (the consequence) and from underlying reasons the condition exists (causes or contributing factors).

## Common Confusions

It's essential to distinguish hazards from outcomes and causes:

- "Runway excursion" is an **outcome**, not a hazard
- "Runway contaminated with standing water" is a **hazard**
- "Poor training" describes a **cause**
- "Training program excludes abnormal procedures for system X" describes a **hazard**

## Principles of Effective Hazard Identification

1. Hazards arise from system-environment interactions
2. A hazard is a condition — not a failure, consequence, or solution
3. Hazard identification should be structured and repeatable
4. Use FAA-aligned taxonomies for consistency (SM ICG, CICTT, NASA ASRS)

## Sources of Hazard Information

Effective hazard identification draws from multiple sources:

- **Voluntary safety reports** from employees
- **Audit findings** from internal and external reviews
- **Flight data monitoring** trends and exceedances
- **Incident and accident investigations**
- **Industry safety data** and information sharing
- **Operational changes** including new equipment, routes, or procedures

## A Practical 3-Step Method

**Step 1 — Start with System & Environment Description.** Before identifying hazards, you must understand the system being analyzed and its operating environment (covered in our previous post).

**Step 2 — Ask "What Could Reduce the Margin of Safety?"** For each system-environment interaction, systematically consider what conditions could compromise safety margins.

**Step 3 — Write Hazards as Clear, Objective Conditions.** Document each hazard as a specific, observable condition — not as a vague concern or an outcome.

## Building a Sustainable Process

The most effective hazard identification programs are continuous — not periodic. They integrate multiple data sources, encourage open reporting, and connect directly to your Safety Risk Management process for analysis and action.
