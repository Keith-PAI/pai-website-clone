Safety culture encompasses the shared values, attitudes, competencies, and behaviors shaping an organization's commitment to safety. Recognized in FAA AC 120-92D, ICAO Doc 9859, and the research of Dr. James Reason, safety culture is not a checklist or policy — it is an ongoing process.

Every organization has a safety culture, whether intentional or not.

## Dr. James Reason's Safety Culture Shield

Dr. James Reason outlined five essential characteristics of a positive safety culture:

- **Informed Culture** — The organization collects and analyzes relevant data to understand where risks exist
- **Reporting Culture** — People feel safe reporting safety concerns without fear of blame or retaliation
- **Just Culture** — Clear distinction between acceptable errors, at-risk behavior, and reckless conduct
- **Flexible Culture** — The organization can adapt and reconfigure when facing high-tempo or unusual operations
- **Learning Culture** — The organization draws conclusions from safety information and implements reforms

## Shifting Toward an Ideal Safety Culture

Building a stronger safety culture involves:

- Implementing **proactive safety measures** rather than only reacting to events
- Establishing **consistent practices** across all levels of the organization
- Demonstrating **visible leadership commitment** to safety
- Making safety **integral to daily operations**, not a separate activity
- **Celebrating safety achievements** and recognizing positive safety behaviors

## The Role of SMS

SMS provides the structure — policies, processes, and procedures — but culture provides the motivation. The Safety Promotion component of SMS is specifically designed to foster positive safety culture through training, communication, and awareness programs.

## Measuring Culture

While safety culture can't be captured in a single metric, organizations can assess it through employee surveys, reporting rates and quality, audit findings, and observation of daily operations. The trend matters more than any single data point.
