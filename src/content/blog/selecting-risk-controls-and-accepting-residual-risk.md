Risk assessment informs decisions. Risk controls implement them.

## Selecting Safety Risk Controls

Effective controls, according to AC 120–92D, should address root causes rather than symptoms, remain proportional to risk levels, avoid unintended consequences, and be clearly documented with assigned responsibility.

Controls may take various forms: engineering modifications, procedural updates, training improvements, or operational constraints.

**Example — Altitude Deviations:** An operator identifies altitude deviation exposure during high-workload flight phases and implements enhanced altitude-alerting logic plus reinforced callout discipline.

**Example — Runway Excursions:** Following industry contaminated runway incidents, an organization strengthens winter operations training and improves dispatch-to-flightcrew coordination during adverse weather.

## Initial and Residual Risk

Initial risk reflects exposure before controls are implemented. Residual risk reflects exposure after controls are applied. Organizations must determine whether residual risk falls within their documented acceptable range using the same risk matrix applied during initial assessment.

**Example — Unstable Approaches:** An operator detects increased unstable approaches in winter weather with hazardous severity and occasional likelihood. Controls include revised stabilized approach criteria, reinforced go-around expectations, and enhanced flight data monitoring. Management then reassesses likelihood and determines if the new risk level meets acceptance standards.

**Example — Taxiway Incursion Risk:** Industry events highlight taxiway confusion at complex airports. Controls include enhanced taxi briefings, updated airport diagrams, and reinforced surface awareness procedures, followed by management evaluation of remaining exposure against thresholds.

## Determining Acceptability

Organizations typically establish acceptability by:

- Applying documented risk matrices
- Confirming controls reduce risk to predefined thresholds
- Ensuring appropriate management authority approves decisions
- Documenting rationale and assumptions

Accepting residual risk reflects a deliberate, documented decision that remaining exposure falls within acceptable bounds and should remain subject to ongoing safety assurance monitoring.

## Conclusion

14 CFR §5.55 creates an integrated process allowing organizations to analyze risk, develop appropriate controls, evaluate residual exposure, and determine acceptability. Structured decision-making, documentation, and proportional action characterize effective Safety Risk Management.
